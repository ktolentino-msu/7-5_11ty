There is a particular shade of green that does not exist on Earth.

I know this because I am looking at it now.

After six years in transit, I have finally reached my destination: the exoplanet Kepler-442c. From orbit, it appears serene — cloud systems spiraling over broad continents, oceans reflecting a slightly warmer star than our own. The atmospheric readings are promising. Oxygen levels are modest but present. Methane signatures fluctuate in ways that suggest biological activity.

This morning, I descended.

The landing was uneventful, which is to say it was miraculous. The ship settled into a valley bordered by towering flora — structures that resemble trees but grow in spirals, their trunks twisting as if shaped by invisible hands. Their leaves shimmer with that unfamiliar green, a hue leaning toward teal yet undeniably alive.

The gravity here is slightly stronger than Earth’s. My steps feel weighted, deliberate. Each movement reminds me that this world is not mine.

I conducted preliminary soil tests. The composition includes complex organic compounds, some analogous to terrestrial amino acids. The air smells faintly metallic, tinged with something sweet. My visor filtered it until I switched to partial atmospheric intake for a controlled sample. The first breath of alien air is something they don’t prepare you for in training.

It tastes like possibility.

There are sounds here — low vibrations carried through the ground, a distant rustling that persists even when there is no wind. I have yet to identify the source. My instruments record fluctuations in electromagnetic fields near the spiraled trees. Life, but not as we scripted it in our models.

I set up a temporary habitat dome near the landing site. As I secured the final anchor, I felt something I haven’t felt since leaving Earth: anticipation without dread. Out there, between the stars, the future was abstract. Here, it feels tangible.

I walked a short distance from the ship at what I have decided to call dusk. The star here sets in a slow, amber cascade, painting the sky in gradients of rose and copper. For a moment, I removed my helmet entirely within the dome’s breathable range and stood under an alien sunset.

I thought about humanity.

About crowded subways and late-night diners. About sourdough starters bubbling on kitchen counters and children learning the names of planets they might never visit. I thought about how fragile we are, and how stubborn.

If this world supports life — even microbial — it changes everything. It means we are not alone in the grand experiment. It means biology is not a cosmic accident but a tendency.

Tomorrow, I will venture farther into the valley. I will catalog, sample, document. I will be careful.

But tonight, I am allowing myself to feel it: the quiet triumph of standing on soil no human has ever touched.

The universe is vast. It is indifferent.

And still, it grows gardens.
