# The Quiet Between Stars

I used to think silence meant absence.

Back on Earth, silence was rare. It lived in early mornings before traffic and in libraries between coughs. Silence was something you could interrupt. Out here, silence is the baseline condition of existence. It is not the absence of sound — it is the absence of everything.

I am currently 14.2 light-years from the nearest human-made object. The ship’s AI hums softly behind the walls, a synthetic heartbeat designed to keep me company. It tells me radiation levels, trajectory drift, protein culture growth rates. It does not tell me how small I feel.

When I look out the observation port, I see stars that have already died. Their light is old by the time it reaches me — relic photons, ancient messengers. I wonder if Earth looks the same from here: a pale blue rumor wrapped in atmosphere and memory.

I record these entries mostly for myself. Transmission lag makes live conversation impossible, and by the time my messages reach home, months will have passed for them and for me. I imagine some archivist in a climate-controlled room, sipping coffee and skimming my dispatches between meetings. I hope they pause sometimes. I hope they imagine what it feels like to float between suns.

Today I performed the standard hull inspection. Microfractures along the starboard plating have stabilized. The self-healing polymer is holding. I spent an hour drifting outside the ship, tethered, watching the void stretch in all directions. No up. No down. Just infinite potential and infinite indifference.

You would think fear would be my constant companion. It isn’t. Fear requires immediacy — a predator, a precipice. Out here, danger is statistical. It lives in probability curves and long-term exposure. Instead of fear, I feel something closer to awe.

The universe does not notice me. It does not care that I crossed it. It does not applaud my courage or mourn my isolation. It simply expands.

And yet I am here.

A collection of carbon and water and borrowed starlight, propelled by equations scratched onto chalkboards centuries ago. I carry with me seeds, data banks, music files, a single pressed daisy sealed in resin. Proof that humanity once looked at fragile things and decided they mattered.

Some nights — if you can call them nights when there is no sun to set — I dim the interior lights and let the stars fill the cabin. I imagine I am swimming through a cosmic ocean. I pretend the constellations are familiar, though I know they are distorted from this angle.

I left Earth to find something new: habitable worlds, microbial life, a second chance for our species. What I have found so far is perspective.

We are so small. Our wars, our arguments, our deadlines — they evaporate against this backdrop. And yet, the fact that we dared to come here at all feels enormous.

If anyone is reading this decades from now, know this: the silence is vast, but it is not empty.

It contains us.
